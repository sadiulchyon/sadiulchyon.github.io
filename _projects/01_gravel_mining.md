Working with **Jim Best** and **Shah Md Atiqul Haq**, I studied how decades of sand and gravel mining reshape river channels in Northeast Bangladesh, and how the landscape and local communities recover once mining stops.

Using a mixed-methods approach that combines remote sensing analysis of four distinct mining periods with interviews of local residents, we found that the active channel recovers faster than abandoned channels and the floodplain, and that extraction sometimes exceeds the river's natural sediment supply. Residents and environmental groups often see the aftermath very differently, with residents reporting lasting hardships long after mining ends.

The work led to six policy recommendations for more sustainable sediment extraction in Bangladesh and comparable settings: supporting artisanal and small-scale mining, rotational extraction zones with monitoring, empowering community involvement, prioritizing extraction outside the active channel, and restoring abandoned channels and floodplain areas.

Published in <a href="https://doi.org/10.1016/j.crsus.2025.100605">Cell Reports Sustainability (2026)</a>.
